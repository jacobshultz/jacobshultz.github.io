Unified Sound Tester

for Drive DeVilbiss Healthcare - May 2025 to August 2025

DeVilbiss Healthcare is a dynamic and innovative leader in respiratory medical equipment with over 135 years of expertise. Its headquarters and manufacturing campus in Somerset hums with energy as dozens of teams across multiple disiplines design and build world-class oxygen concentrators, nebulizers, suction units, and sleep-therapy solutions that improve lives across more than 100 countries. Backed by the resources of Drive, the Somerset facility continues to expand production and help their customers breathe easier!

The Unified Sound Tester is an application used daily on the production line to verify whether each unit meets acceptable volume standards during operation. Prior to this project, the sound-testing process relied on three separate applications. The goal was to consolidate them into a single, streamlined tool to increase production efficiency, extend long-term maintainability, and improve scalability.

At DeVilbiss, sound testing is a regular part of the production line assembly process. Each unit is checked to make sure its noise levels stay within set limits. The first program, built in VB6, handled just three oxygen concentrator models. As production expanded and new models came out, it didn't make sense to support the original sound tester application written in ancient VB6, so fancy new .NET apps were made to accommodate each of the new models.

This solution, while working temporarily, was not without its issues. To begin, assemblers on the factory floor were confused with multiple apps. To them, a sound test is just a sound test. Why do they need to open a new program depending on the unit? Additionally, the SwEs at DeVilbiss knew that as production expanded this multi-app approach would become unmaintainable. The software team had built several programs that all do almost exactly the same thing. If ten new concentrator models are added to production, will ten new apps be created? The assemblers definitely won't like that! Not to mention, if something should change with how sound testing was performed, how long will it take to get ten (identical) changes implemented across all the apps? For these reasons, the SwE team at DeVilbiss recognized the need for a new application that could support sound testing for every model of oxygen concentrator on the line, that could "Unify" the three existing sound testers.

As part of my summer internship with Drive DeVilbiss, I was to build the Unified Sound Tester, an application which would consolidate the three existing sound testing applications into one unified piece of software. With the tool I built, testing is simple and streamlined: scan the unit's serial number, enter its decibel reading, and the app automatically routes the results to the right database all in one seamless flow. As of writing, it supports every existing oxygen concentrator model. It's designed for easy expansion, so the software team barely has to lift a finger to accommodate new models as they're released.

I started by implementing the UI in a WinForms project, which was pretty straightforward. The real challenge came with the backend: I needed to design a system that could easily expand as new models were added. On top of that, I had to integrate my work with the company's existing codebase and database systems, which meant diving into and understanding code and tables I hadn't created. The database systems at DeVilbiss are complex. I had to have unique CRUD logic for each model. By the end of the summer I had added numerous contributions to the codebase to make existing modules work for my project. Thankfully, I wasn't alone — the software team supported me throughout the entire development process. They guided me from sketching the UI on paper to building my project's backend to setting up an inheritance structure for scalability and, finally, writing documentation and testing protocols for my software.

The software team at DeVilbiss showed me what it is to be a professional. The engineers there are exceptional at what they do, they are dedicated to their work, and they are kind and generous to a fault. It is so rare to work with a group of people who are all passionate about what they do and get along with one another. Who are skilled enough to tackle whatever problem may come their way, but are humble enough to be able to admit when they are wrong. Working at Drive DeVilbiss gave me a great appreciation for working on an efficient team and was my first experience collaborating with others to build software. It was also a great trial-by-fire of working with databases and gave me some backend chops.

SKILLS: .NET, MS SQL Server, Git/GitHub, MS Access, Winforms, Software Testing, Documentation.

Manual Generator

for GAPVAX - June 2024 to November 2024

GapVax, Inc. is a world-renound manufacturer of custom industrial and municipal vacuum equipment, headquartered in Johnstown, Pennsylvania. Their vehicles are used for tasks like cleaning industrial debris, safely digging around buried utilities, and maintaining municipal sewer systems. Each unit is custom-built to client specifications, with a focus on durability, ease of maintenance, and efficient operation. What sets GapVax apart is its commitment to quality and support. With over 130 employees and an additional plant in Texas, GapVax remains a leader in the vacuum truck industry by blending customization, rugged design, and responsive customer support. Nothing beats a GapVax!

The Section 4 Template Generator—or "Manual Generator" as everyone else calls it—is a tool I built to streamline the creation of owner's manuals for GapVax trucks. I used OLE automation to control an instance of Adobe Acrobat within Microsoft Excel, using Excel workbooks to handle user input and Acrobat to edit and save PDFs.

GapVax designs and builds highly customized vacuum trucks, and each one ships with a detailed owner's manual. In them section 4 "Operation" covers how to use every piece of equipment on the truck, but because no two trucks are exactly alike, each truck would require a unique section 4 that would need to be created by hand.

While that level of customization showcases GapVax's quality, it makes manual creation slow and labor-intensive; by the time I joined, production had scaled dramatically, and the old approach to Section 4 had become a serious bottleneck.

When using the tool I developed, the user is presented with a list of optional equipment features for a specific truck model. They can then select the desired features from the customer's order, and the program generates a customized 'section 4 template' of documentation based on those selections. While some adjustments may still be necessary before the final document is ready to be included with final manual for the truck, the tool significantly reduces the hand-effort by automatically assembling the relevant content starting points for the chosen options—essentially handling the tedious "gluing together" of documentation for optional equipment features. This is where the bloated name "Section 4 Template Generator" comes from.

If you're familiar with software development you may be thinking that using Excel as a "pseudo-platform" is a strange, backwards idea. I did too. When presented with the manual creation problem I originally recommended building a Python app. However, leadership ultimately decided everything would be done in Excel. While definitely not typical, it actually fit their needs very cleverly: they needed a solution requiring almost no maintenance after I left, one that was easy to distribute without an installer, and something staff already knew how to use. Excel fit all three—simple to share, backwards-compatible, and familiar to non-technical users.

I separated the app into multiple front ends and a singular back end: Each front end was a workbook dedicated to UI, it contained all the buttons and configuration for the listed options and contained no code. I made 'master copy' front end workbooks for each model of GapVax trucks. When a user needs to use the 'Manual Generator' for a MC Series unit (for example), they can simply copy the master UI workbook into a directory of their liking. From there, they can modify, duplicate, share, delete, or move the excel file without breaking the functionality of their copied workbook or any other manual generator front end workbooks. This is because the back end of the application (a separate, singular 'library' workbook) contained all the code, userforms, additional configuration, and ran invisibly in the background. Each front end workbook connects seamlessly to the singular back end workbook over the company's file system. The front end workbooks only have to make a singular call to the backend's API, which is designed to work with any front end workbook that follows the standard format I designed.

I also built the app to be expanded upon without having to write code or edit configuration files. As I just mentioned, the backend can run on any frontend that is configured correctly. This way, should more models of trucks be added to production, all that needs to be done is the addition of a new frontend workbook to reflect the options for the new truck model (an existing frontend workbook can be easily duped and configured to the specifications of the new model). Most of the configuration is done within each unique instance of UI workbook in a hidden sheet. From there, users can add new selectable options directly in the workbook by inserting rows and editing columns, or by using the wizard I included.

The biggest challenge in this project was working within the constraints of Excel's scripting language, VBA. While it technically supports classes, it lacks core object-oriented features like inheritance and polymorphism, as well as modern conveniences such as exception handling (yes, I had to use On Error GoTo). Despite these limitations, I still managed to implement using the MVP pattern and a scalable front/back end split. This was also summer of Freshman year; my first time developing a project for industry. Sticking to software engineering principles required extensive research. In the end, I gained a deep appreciation for why OOP paradigms exist, along with valuable experience in gathering requirements, creating UIs for non-technical users, and designing backend systems.

SKILLS: VBScript, Microsoft Excel Scripting, UI Design, Backend Design, Requirement Gathering, Creative Problem Solving.
