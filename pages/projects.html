Unified Sound Tester

for Drive DeVilbiss Healthcare - May 2025 to August 2025

DeVilbiss Healthcare is a dynamic and innovative leader in respiratory medical equipment with over 135 years of expertise. Its headquarters and manufacturing campus in Somerset hums with energy as dozens of teams across multiple disiplines design and build world-class oxygen concentrators, nebulizers, suction units, and sleep-therapy solutions that improve lives across more than 100 countries. Backed by the resources of Drive, the Somerset facility continues to expand production and help their customers breathe easier!

The Unified Sound Tester is an application used daily on the production line to verify whether each unit meets acceptable volume standards during operation. Prior to this project, the sound-testing process relied on three separate applications. The goal was to consolidate them into a single, streamlined tool to increase production efficiency, extend long-term maintainability, and improve scalability.

At DeVilbiss, sound testing is a regular part of the production line assembly process. Each unit is checked to make sure its noise levels stay within set limits. The first program, built in VB6, handled just three oxygen concentrator models. As production expanded and new models came out, it didn't make sense to support the original sound tester application written in ancient VB6, so fancy new .NET apps were made to accommodate each of the new models. This left the software team with several programs that all do almost exactly the same thing.

This solution, while working temporarily, was not without it's issues. To begin, assemblers on the factory floor were confused with multiple apps. To them, a sound test is just a sound test. Why do they need to open a new program depending on the unit? Additionally, the SwEs at DeVilbiss knew that as production expanded this multi-app approach would become unmaintainable. If ten new models are added to production, will ten new apps be created? The assemblers definitely won't like that! Not to mention, if something should change with how sound testing was performed, how long will it take to get ten (identical) changes implemented in all the apps? For these reasons, the SwE team at DeVilbiss recognized the need for a new application that could support sound testing for every model of oxygen concentrator on the line, that could "Unify" the three existing sound testers.

As part of my summer internship with Drive DeVilbiss, I was to build the Unified Sound Tester, an application which would consolidate the three existing sound testing applications into one unified piece of software. With the tool I built, testing is simple and streamlined: scan the unit's serial number, enter its decibel reading, and the app automatically routes the results to the right database — all in one seamless flow. As of writing, it supports every existing oxygen concentrator model — and it's designed for easy expansion, so the software team barely has to lift a finger to accommodate new models as they're released.

I started by implementing the UI in a WinForms project, which was pretty straightforward. The real challenge came with the backend: I needed to design a system that could easily expand as new models were added. On top of that, I had to integrate my work with the company's existing codebase and database systems, which meant diving into and understanding code and tables I hadn't created. Thankfully, I wasn't alone — the software team supported me throughout the entire development process. They guided me from sketching the UI to building my project's backend to setting up an inheritance structure for scalability and, finally, writing documentation and testing protocols for my software.

I could write many paragraphs about the things I learned at Drive DeVilbiss. Software things, business things... But the most important thing I learned in my time there was the value of a high-functioning, caring team. Seriously, the SwE team at DeVilbiss are a group of rock-stars. They are exceptional at what they do, they are dedicated to their work, and (most importantly for nit-wit interns like myself) they are exceptionally kind and generous. It is so rare to work with a group of people who are all passionate about what they do and get along with one another. Who are skilled enough to tackle whatever problem may come their way, but are humble enough to be able to admit when they are wrong. Working on that team showed me what it is to be a professional. I can only hope one day I will get to be part of a team half as well-oiled and kind as they were!

SKILLS: .NET, SQL Server, Git/GitHub, MS Access, Winforms, Software Testing, Documentation.

Manual Generator

for GAPVAX - June 2024 to November 2024

GapVax, Inc. is a world-renound manufacturer of custom industrial and municipal vacuum equipment, headquartered in Johnstown, Pennsylvania. Their vehicles are used for tasks like cleaning industrial debris, safely digging around buried utilities, and maintaining municipal sewer systems. Each unit is custom-built to client specifications, with a focus on durability, ease of maintenance, and efficient operation. What sets GapVax apart is its commitment to quality and support. With over 130 employees and an additional plant in Texas, GapVax remains a leader in the vacuum truck industry by blending customization, rugged design, and responsive customer support. Nothing beats a GapVax!

The Section 4 Template Generator—or "Manual Generator" as everyone else calls it—is a tool I built to streamline the creation of owner's manuals for GapVax trucks. I used OLE automation to control an instance of Adobe Acrobat within Microsoft Excel, using Excel workbooks to handle user input and Acrobat to edit and save PDFs.

GapVax designs and builds highly customized vacuum trucks, and each one ships with a detailed owner's manual. In them section 4—Operation—covers how to use every piece of equipment on the truck, but because no two trucks are exactly alike, each truck would require a unique section 4 that would need to be created by hand.

While that level of customization showcases GapVax's quality, it makes manual creation slow and labor-intensive; by the time I joined, production had scaled dramatically, and the old approach to Section 4 had become a serious bottleneck.

When using the tool I developed, the user is presented with a list of available options for a specific truck model. They can then select the desired features, and the program generates a customized 'template' of documentation based on those selections. While some adjustments may still be necessary before the final document is ready to ship with the truck, the tool significantly reduces the hand-effort by automatically assembling the relevant content starting points for the chosen options—essentially handling the tedious "gluing together" of documentation for optional features. This is where the bloated name "Section 4 Template Generator" comes from.

If you're familiar with software you may be thinking that using Excel as a "pseudo-platform" is a strange, backwards idea. I did too. When presented with the manual creation problem I originally recommended building a Python app. However leadership ultimately decided everything would be done in Excel. While definitely not typical, it actually fit their needs very cleverly: they needed a solution requiring almost no maintenance after I left, one that was easy to distribute without installers, and something staff already knew how to use. Excel fit all three—simple to share, backwards-compatible, and familiar to non-technical users.

I separated the app into a front end and back end: The front end was just a UI workbook with all the buttons and configuration for the listed options and contained no code, while the back end (another workbook) contained all the code, userforms, additional configuration, and ran invisibly in the background. This setup let users move, delete, share, or duplicate a front end workbook without breaking functionality, as it connected seamlessly to the back end over the company's file system. I built the app to be expanded upon without having to write code or edit configuration files. Configuration is done within the actual workbook in a hidden sheet. From there, users can add new options directly in the workbook by inserting rows and editing columns. I even added a wizard for configuring the configuration sheet!

The biggest challenge in this project was working within the constraints of Excel's scripting language, VBA. While it technically supports classes, it lacks core object-oriented features like inheritance and polymorphism, as well as modern conveniences such as exception handling (yes, I had to use On Error GoTo). Despite these limitations, I still managed to implement using the MVP pattern. It was also my first time developing a project for industry, so sticking to software engineering principles required extensive research. In the end, I gained a deep appreciation for why OOP paradigms exist, along with valuable experience in gathering requirements and creating UIs for non-technical users.

SKILLS: VBScript, Microsoft Excel Scripting, UI Design, Requirement Gathering.
